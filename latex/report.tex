\section{General Results}
%% ======================================================================

This report compares three methods for extracting Dysonian EPR line
parameters ($B_0$, $\Delta B$, $p$) from first-derivative experimental
spectra recorded on Bruker spectrometers:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{MATLAB optimizer} --- \texttt{lsqnonlin} (9~parameters:
          $B_0$, $\Delta B$, $p$, $A$, offset, slope, quad, $B_\mathrm{scale}$,
          $B_\mathrm{shift}$) with \texttt{fminsearch} fallback.
    \item \textbf{EasySpin esfit} --- Levenberg--Marquardt, two-step masked
          approach (step~1: fix~$p$; step~2: fix baseline).
    \item \textbf{1D Residual CNN} --- trained on synthetic Dysonian spectra,
          predicts $B_0$/$\Delta B$/$p$ directly from the normalized spectrum.
\end{enumerate}

All three use the same Feher--Kip Dysonian model with the full two-term
$A$/$D$ coefficients (Holiatkina et~al., \textit{J.~Appl.~Phys.}
\textbf{134}, 145702, 2023). Total: \textbf{180~experimental spectra}
across 5~independent sets.

\subsection{Key Advantages of the CNN Approach}

\begin{description}
    \item[No initial parameter estimates required.]
    Classical iterative methods depend critically on starting guesses.
    In our data, MATLAB returned $p=0.200$ (lower bound) for 7/53 spectra
    in set-3, and EasySpin returned $p=5.000$ (upper bound) for 2~spectra
    --- convergence failures affecting 10--15\% of fits. The CNN never
    produces such artifacts.

    \item[Speed enables high-throughput analysis.]
    The CNN processes a spectrum in $\sim$0.1\,s (single forward pass)
    compared to 1--5\,s for MATLAB and 5--30\,s for EasySpin. Processing
    80~spectra of set-4 takes $\sim$8\,s with CNN vs.\ $\sim$15\,min with
    EasySpin --- enabling real-time analysis during EPR experiments.

    \item[Deterministic reproducibility.]
    The same spectrum always produces the same parameters. MATLAB and
    EasySpin disagree by $\Delta B_0 \approx 15$--20\,G on identical
    spectra due to different optimization strategies.

    \item[No licensed toolbox dependencies.]
    CNN inference requires only open-source libraries (NumPy, TensorFlow).
\end{description}

\section{Additional Advantages of the CNN Approach}

\subsection{Robustness to Convergence Failures}

Classical optimization methods are susceptible to convergence failures,
particularly for spectra with low signal-to-noise ratio or narrow linewidths.
In our dataset:

\begin{itemize}
    \item MATLAB \texttt{lsqnonlin} hit the lower bound ($p = 0.200$) on
          7 out of 53 spectra in set-3 and 5 out of 80 in set-4.
    \item EasySpin \texttt{esfit} diverged to the upper bound ($p = 5.000$)
          on 2 spectra in set-3, and returned $\Delta B = 5.0$\,G (lower bound)
          on several narrow-line spectra.
    \item The CNN returned physically meaningful values for all 180 spectra
          without exception.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Processing Speed Comparison}

\begin{table}[H]
\centering

The CNN achieves a \textbf{30--100$\times$ speedup} over classical methods.
This makes it practical for:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Real-time parameter monitoring during temperature or power sweeps.
    \item High-throughput screening of large spectral datasets.
    \item Spatial parameter mapping in EPR imaging experiments.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Reproducibility}

Classical methods produce different results depending on:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Initial parameter estimates (heuristic, implementation-dependent).
    \item Optimization strategy (MATLAB's 9-parameter simultaneous fit
          vs.\ EasySpin's 2-step masked approach).
    \item Convergence tolerances and iteration limits.
\end{itemize}

In our data, MATLAB and EasySpin disagree by $\Delta B_0 \approx 15$--20\,G
on the same spectra across all narrow-line sets. This systematic offset
arises because MATLAB fits $B_\mathrm{scale}$ and $B_\mathrm{shift}$
calibration parameters that absorb field offsets, while EasySpin fixes
$B_\mathrm{scale} = 1$, $B_\mathrm{shift} = 0$.

The CNN produces deterministic, reproducible results: the same input
spectrum always yields the same output parameters, independent of
initialization or optimizer settings.

\subsection{Limitations and Trade-offs}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Pre-training required:} Each parameter regime requires
          a dedicated model ($\sim$5\,min training on Google Colab T4 GPU).
    \item \textbf{No uncertainty estimates:} The CNN returns point estimates
          only; classical methods provide confidence intervals.
    \item \textbf{Bounded generalization:} Spectra outside the training
          parameter ranges produce unreliable predictions without warning.
    \item \textbf{Magnetic window sensitivity:} The CNN's B-axis must match
          the experimental sweep exactly to avoid zero-filled artifacts.
\end{itemize}
